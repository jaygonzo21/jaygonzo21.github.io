"This is Water"

by David Foster Wallace


Logos

In "This Is Water," David Foster Wallace employs logos by using logical reasoning to illustrate the importance of awareness and conscious choice in daily life. He presents relatable scenarios—like the monotony of adult routines and the automatic nature of thoughts—arguing that our default perceptions can lead to unexamined lives. By logically explaining how choosing to see beyond our own experiences can enhance empathy and understanding, he encourages readers to recognize their ability to control their perceptions and responses, ultimately advocating for a more mindful existence.

Pathos

In "This Is Water," David Foster Wallace uses pathos to evoke emotional responses and connect with his audience on a personal level. He shares anecdotes about the struggles of adult life, such as the frustration of daily routines and the feelings of isolation and existential dread. By addressing the emotional weight of these experiences, he resonates with the audience’s own feelings of disconnection and helplessness. His emphasis on empathy and awareness encourages readers to feel compassion for themselves and others, making a powerful case for living a more deliberate and connected life.

Ethos

In "This Is Water," David Foster Wallace establishes ethos by drawing on his credibility as a speaker and thinker. He shares personal anecdotes and reflections from his own life, particularly his experiences in college and as a writer. By acknowledging his own struggles with awareness and empathy, he positions himself as someone who has grappled with the same challenges as his audience. This honesty and vulnerability enhance his authority and trustworthiness, making his insights more relatable and impactful. By demonstrating genuine concern for the human experience, he invites readers to trust his perspective on living mindfully.

The Default Setting

The default setting is an unconscience and automatic way of thinking. We as humans natrually view ourselves as the center of the universe, because we see everything in the first person. This means we have a self-centered point of view and always place our own struggles above the struggles of others. We get up everyday to do the same things, the same morning routine, brushing our teeth, putting on our shoes the exact same way we did the day before, the exact same foot first. We drive to work or school the exact same way, and we get frustrated at the same never ending traffic that lies in front of us.

1. Awareness and Consciousness

In order to escape the default setting, you have to be aware that you are running on "autopilot". Running on our default setting isn't wrong, it's just the way our brains were made to function. However, it is important to practice escaping the default setting so that we aren't limited by our own biases.

2. Choice and Discipline

Wallace says that although the default setting is automatic, we have the freedom to choose how to respond to the world. Making that choice to act against our default setting is difficult, since the default setting is easy and saves energy.


3. Empathy and Perspective

One of the most important ways to break free from the default setting is to try to be more empathatic towards others. If try your hardest to put yourself into someone else's shoes, you will realize that everyone is dealing with their own problems and struggles.

4. Living with Intention

If you break free from the default setting, you begin to choose how you want to live and interpret your life. Rather than letting the mind automatically drift to thoughts of ego, frustration, or complaint, you can deliberately focus on what truly matters, such as kindness, gratitude, and understanding.
